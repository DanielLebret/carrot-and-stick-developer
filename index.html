00 · WHO REDEVELOPS AND WHEN

The model predicts your pipeline

Before evaluating what policy does, it helps to know which sites the research predicts will redevelop in the first place. The model is calibrated to every residential parcel in NYC. The sites with the highest predicted redevelopment probability share three characteristics: old buildings, significant unused density relative to the zoning envelope, and strong underlying rent potential.

That profile will be familiar to any development professional actively sourcing sites in NYC. A 1960s walk-up in Astoria sitting at 30% of its allowable FAR in a submarket with rising rents is exactly the kind of parcel the model flags as a high-probability redevelopment candidate. A recently renovated building in the Bronx already built to its zoning limit is not.

The heatmap below makes this explicit. Redevelopment probability rises sharply as buildings age and as unused capacity increases. The interaction matters: an old building already built to its FAR has limited redevelopment upside. An underbuilt building in a high-rent submarket is where the model concentrates predicted activity.

The spatial implication is that predicted redevelopment concentrates in Western Brooklyn, Queens, and parts of Staten Island — not Manhattan, where most parcels are already built close to their zoning limits. If your pipeline is concentrated in those outer-borough submarkets with aging, underbuilt stock, this research is calibrated directly to your deal flow.

01 · THE PUZZLE

More units, higher rents. Here is why that is not a contradiction.

NYC has added significant housing stock over the past decade. Rents have kept climbing. The standard supply-and-demand intuition says this should not happen. In practice it does, and understanding why is essential for anticipating how your projects affect — and are affected by — the broader market.

The answer is not that supply does not matter. It is that in a built-out city, new supply does not arrive on an empty margin. Every new building replaces something. And what it replaces is typically older, physically depreciated stock that was renting at a discount relative to the market. When you demolish a six-story walk-up and build a 20-story tower, you are not just adding 14 stories of net new supply. You are removing discounted units and replacing them with undepreciated units that enter the market at full current rents.

The net effect on average rents depends on which force dominates: the downward pressure from more units, or the upward pressure from the composition shift toward higher-quality stock. In NYC, at empirically calibrated supply elasticities, the composition shift dominates.

This has a direct implication for how you think about market positioning. Your new development is not competing primarily with the building you demolished. It is entering a market where the average rent is being pulled upward by the same redevelopment activity driving your own project.

02 · THE MECHANISM

The replacement effect: what it means for your pro forma

The replacement effect is not an abstract research finding. It is the reason that markets where significant redevelopment activity is concentrated tend to see stronger rent growth than markets where the existing stock turns over slowly.

The mechanism works through three channels simultaneously. First, your new building enters without accumulated depreciation — it commands full market rent from day one rather than the discounted rent that characterized the aging structure it replaced. Second, new construction in your neighborhood raises local amenity values, putting upward pressure on rents for nearby properties as well. Third, the aggregate supply increase exerts downward pressure on citywide rents, though at NYC's calibrated demand elasticity this effect is smaller than the first two.

For a development professional, the practical implication is this: markets where redevelopment is accelerating are markets where your stabilized rents are likely to outperform projections anchored to current comparables, because the comparables themselves are being pulled upward by the same activity driving your project.

03 · CARROTS

Tax exemptions and density bonuses do different things to your deal

Tax exemptions work on your go/no-go threshold.

By reducing post-construction tax liability, a tax exemption makes a project pencil out at a lower stabilized rent. It pulls forward projects that would otherwise sit on the margin for years waiting for rents to rise enough to justify construction. The effect is broad: it raises redevelopment probabilities across a wide range of parcel types, concentrating new construction in areas with older buildings and significant unused density.

Figure I · Panel C — Tax Exemption Length (τ) — Both property value and development probability increase monotonically with exemption length across all demand levels. A 20-year exemption raises redevelopment probability by approximately 15 percentage points on a typical marginal parcel.

Figure I · Panel C — Tax Exemption Length (τ) — Both property value and development probability increase monotonically with exemption length across all demand levels. A 20-year exemption raises redevelopment probability by approximately 15 percentage points on a typical marginal parcel.

The fiscal tradeoff is real. The city foregoes property tax revenues during the exemption window. But from your perspective as a developer, a 20-year exemption raises your project's redevelopment probability by approximately 15 percentage points on a typical marginal parcel, and by more than 20 percentage points on parcels already close to their redevelopment threshold.

Density bonuses work on your optimal project scale — and may cause you to wait.

A density bonus raises the value of your development option by allowing a larger building. But it also raises your optimal rent threshold, because the larger project is only worth building once rents are high enough to justify the higher construction cost. If you are near your redevelopment threshold today, a density bonus may actually cause you to delay: you will rationally wait for rents to reach the level that justifies the taller building rather than proceeding now at lower density.

Figure I · Panel B — Density Bonuses (φ) — Property value rises monotonically with density bonus, but development probability peaks and then declines as developers optimally wait for higher rents to justify larger projects. The effect is strongest on parcels near their redevelopment threshold.

Figure I · Panel B — Density Bonuses (φ) — Property value rises monotonically with density bonus, but development probability peaks and then declines as developers optimally wait for higher rents to justify larger projects. The effect is strongest on parcels near their redevelopment threshold.

This is not a theoretical prediction. It is a direct implication of the optimization: the value of the density bonus is only realized at higher density, and higher density requires higher stabilized rents to cover convex construction costs. At the baseline construction cost elasticity calibrated to NYC data, a 50% density bonus raises property values by approximately 6% on a typical parcel, but actually reduces near-term redevelopment probability on parcels near the threshold as developers wait to build taller.

The spatial implication matters for site selection.

Tax exemptions generate the broadest redevelopment response, concentrating in outer-borough neighborhoods with aging stock and unused FAR. Density bonuses concentrate gains in higher-value locations where the larger building is most profitable.

If your pipeline is in Western Brooklyn or Queens in older, underbuilt neighborhoods, tax exemptions are the more powerful tool. If you are targeting high-value Manhattan-adjacent submarkets where rents can support a taller building, density bonuses add more value.

Stacking both instruments produces the largest gains.

The combined incentive policy — tax exemption plus density bonus plus affordability mandate — is the only policy that expands both affordable and market-rate units simultaneously relative to baseline. No single instrument achieves this. The tax exemption pulls forward the timing decision while the density bonus increases the scale of the project that gets built.

04 · STICKS

What an affordability mandate actually costs your project

Affordability mandates are the most direct instrument for producing below-market units. They are also the instrument that most directly affects your project economics. Understanding exactly what a mandate does — and does not do — to your deal is essential for evaluating any Carrot and Stick program.

What a mandate does to your threshold.

A mandate reduces your net rental income by requiring a share of units to rent below market. This raises the rent level at which redevelopment becomes optimal: you need rents to be higher before the project pencils out. The effect is strongest on high-demand parcels, where the rental discount on affordable units represents a larger absolute dollar amount, and on parcels near their redevelopment threshold, where the mandate most tightly constrains the economics.

Figure I · Panel A — Affordable Mandate (α) — Both property value and development probability decline monotonically as the mandate share increases, with stronger effects in high-demand areas. A 30% mandate reduces 10-year redevelopment probability by 2–5 percentage points on a typical parcel near its threshold.

Figure I · Panel A — Affordable Mandate (α) — Both property value and development probability decline monotonically as the mandate share increases, with stronger effects in high-demand areas. A 30% mandate reduces 10-year redevelopment probability by 2–5 percentage points on a typical parcel near its threshold.

What a mandate does not do.

The units your mandate produces do not reach the bottom of the NYC rent distribution. Affordable units produced through inclusionary requirements tend to appear in new buildings on higher-value parcels, because those are the projects where redevelopment still pencils out despite the requirement. At a 25% discounted rent, the affordable units you produce will typically be above the median rent of the existing stock in lower-income neighborhoods.

The voluntary adoption decision.

If the mandate is voluntary — paired with incentives that developers can choose to accept or decline — approximately 46% of NYC parcels would opt into a Density Incentive Zoning program and 46% into a Fiscal Incentive Zoning program at moderate affordability levels. When both incentives are combined, adoption rises to 74%.

05 · THE COST

Who pays for each mandated affordable unit — and how the instrument determines the split

Every mandated affordable unit represents a gap between the private return and the social objective. That gap has to be absorbed somewhere. Our analysis makes explicit where it goes — and the answer depends entirely on which incentive instrument accompanies the mandate.

Each mandated affordable unit carries an estimated cost of approximately $60,000 under density-based policies and $64,000 under fiscal policies, measured as the combined reduction in land values and foregone tax revenues per affordable unit produced while holding total supply fixed.

Under tax exemptions paired with mandates:

Up to 90% of the cost falls on the public sector through foregone tax revenues. Landowners are largely held harmless. From your perspective as a developer, this is the most favorable structure: the city absorbs the affordability cost through its tax base rather than through your land value.

Under density bonuses paired with mandates:

The cost splits roughly evenly. Landowners absorb approximately $30,000 per unit through lower land value capitalization. The public sector absorbs a comparable amount. Neither side escapes unscathed, but neither bears the full cost.

The practical implication for negotiating with the city:

If you are in a negotiation over an inclusionary program, the instrument matters as much as the mandate share. A 30% mandate paired with a 20-year tax exemption is a fundamentally different deal than a 30% mandate paired with a 50% density bonus, even if both produce the same number of affordable units citywide. The former concentrates cost on the public sector. The latter splits it with landowners.

06 · THE RENOVATION DECISION

When does it make more sense to renovate than redevelop?

The model allows developers to choose between renovation and full redevelopment. The results are clear about when each option dominates.

Renovation is the preferred option only when a building is already close to its zoning limit. Below 75% of capacity utilization, virtually no parcel in the model prefers renovation over redevelopment. The renovation option becomes relevant above 85% of capacity and dominates above 95%.

Figure E1 · Renovation Decision and Probability of Action by Used Capacity — Renovation (red circles) only dominates redevelopment above 85–95% of capacity utilization. Below 75%, virtually no parcel prefers renovation.

Figure E1 · Renovation Decision and Probability of Action by Used Capacity — Renovation (red circles) only dominates redevelopment above 85–95% of capacity utilization. Below 75%, virtually no parcel prefers renovation.

The intuition is straightforward. Redevelopment is valuable when it allows you to build significantly more density than currently exists. When the existing building is already close to the FAR limit, the density gain from demolition and rebuild is small, and the foregone rental income during a multi-year construction period is not worth it. Renovation restores quality without that cost.

For site selection, this implies a concrete rule: if a site is above 85% of its allowable FAR, your underwriting should model renovation economics rather than redevelopment economics. Below 75% of FAR utilization, redevelopment dominates unless construction costs are unusually high relative to stabilized rents.

One important caveat: affordability mandates do not apply to renovation in NYC. This creates an opt-out margin. When a mandate is imposed, some developers near the FAR limit will rationally choose renovation over redevelopment to avoid the requirement. The model finds that a 30% mandate increases the share of parcels preferring renovation by approximately 8 percentage points. If you are near the FAR limit and facing a mandatory inclusionary requirement, this tradeoff is worth modeling explicitly.

07 · WHERE TO TARGET

Matching your site selection strategy to the policy environment

The same policy bundle produces very different outcomes depending on where it is applied. Targeting strategy matters as much as policy design.

High-Demand Parcels

Manhattan · Western Brooklyn · Western Queens

Maximum unit production, strongest rent appreciation, highest fiscal cost. If your strategy is to maximize project scale and you can underwrite the affordability requirement, high-demand targeting delivers the largest absolute numbers. The replacement effect is strongest here: average rents rise most sharply because the quality gap between demolished and new stock is largest.

Targeting high-demand areas maximizes unit production but generates the strongest replacement-driven rent increases. The fiscal cost is also highest here.

High-Obsolescence Parcels

Brooklyn · Bronx · Queens

Moderate unit production, smallest rent increases of any strategy. Replacing the worst buildings compresses the quality gap between old and new stock, moderating the replacement effect. If your strategy is oriented toward workforce housing or moderate-income markets, obsolescence-targeted redevelopment produces the most favorable rent dynamics.

Targeting obsolete buildings moderates the replacement effect. Rent increases are roughly half those seen in high-demand areas under equivalent policies.

High Unused-Capacity Parcels

Bronx · Staten Island · Eastern Queens

The only targeting scenario where supply expansion actually reduces average rents. In lower-demand areas with substantial unused FAR, the traditional supply mechanism works as expected. Unit production is strong under fiscal incentive zoning.

08 · IMPLICATIONS

Three things every NYC developer should know about the current policy environment

01

Stack the tools

No single instrument achieves what the combination achieves. Tax exemptions accelerate your timing decision. Density bonuses increase your optimal project scale. Paired together with an affordability mandate, they are the only combination that expands both market-rate and affordable units simultaneously. If you have the option to participate in a combined program, the model suggests it dominates participating in any single-instrument program.

02

Match the instrument to the site

Tax exemptions add the most value on older, underbuilt sites in outer-borough submarkets where rents are approaching but have not yet cleared the redevelopment threshold. Density bonuses add the most value on high-demand sites where a taller building is profitable and the FAR increase translates directly into additional revenue. Applying a density bonus to a marginal site in a lower-demand submarket may actually cause you to delay a project that would otherwise proceed.

03

Do not expect mandates to reach the lowest-income households

The affordable units your mandate produces will not sit at the bottom of the NYC rent distribution. They will sit in new buildings on higher-value sites, above the median rent of the aging stock that serves the lowest-income households. If your program is designed to address affordability at the very bottom of the market, preserving and improving the existing aging stock is a more direct mechanism than mandating affordability in new construction.
